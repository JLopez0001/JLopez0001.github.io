Introduction

Meet Calm Space, our innovative electronic medical records (EMR) system designed to streamline the healthcare experience. Calm Space offers a dynamic dual-user platform that allows therapists and QA specialists to manage patient profiles, diagnoses, and notes with unprecedented ease and efficiency.

With enhanced UI transparency and a robust Mongoose-based database, every team member stays informed and empowered, ensuring better healthcare outcomes and seamless documentation workflows.

Idea behind Calm Space

Calm Space was conceived from a pressing need I identified during my tenure as a quality assurance specialist in health tech.

Working with existing electronic medical records (EMR) systems, I observed that their complex interfaces led providers to miss crucial alerts, such as note rejections, resulting in significant delays in patient care.

Motivated to enhance this scenario, I developed Calm Space—a system designed with clarity and simplicity to empower healthcare providers and significantly improve the efficiency of healthcare delivery.

UI Experience

Getting Started

To begin using Calm Space, users will first need to sign in with their unique username and password. If they do not have an account, they will be prompted to register, enabling them to access and utilize the full features of the platform.

Therapist home page

Upon signing in, therapists are immediately presented with their patient caseload. Each patient card displays essential demographic information and includes a direct link to the patient's chart, ensuring efficient access to vital data.

Creating a patient

In designing the process to create a patient profile, my priority was simplicity. The user interface requires only the essential demographic information needed for each patient, streamlining the process and enhancing usability.

Patient Chart

Within the patient chart, therapists can effortlessly add notes or diagnoses and view all existing notes, patient status updates, appointment times, and types of service. This comprehensive overview ensures that therapists have all necessary information readily available to enhance patient care.

Creating a note

To create and submit a note for QA review, therapists are required to detail the proceedings of the session, including the goals addressed. This documentation is essential for maintaining comprehensive patient records.

Additionally, the method of service must be specified to clarify how the session was conducted, which is crucial for accurate record-keeping.

Finally, for authentication and security, therapists must re-enter the patient's unique ID, the QA provider code, and their own provider ID before signing and submitting the note.

QA task page

After a note is submitted, it automatically appears in the QA task box. Here, QA specialists evaluate whether the note meets all necessary standards and decide if it should be approved or if revisions are required.

Reviewing a note

A note undergoes a thorough review by QA specialists who assess it against the criteria set by the Department of Mental Health.

Depending on compliance with these standards, the note may be approved or rejected.

If a note is rejected, the QA specialist must provide a specific reason for the rejection. This feedback is then sent back to the therapist, who is required to make the necessary corrections and resubmit the note.


Challenges

Major Challenge on this Project

The primary challenge in this project was managing the feedback process for rejected notes and accurately updating the status to reflect whether notes were rejected, pending, or approved. Fortunately, my background in quality assurance provided a solid foundation for addressing these challenges. Additionally, having access to a visual representation of a functioning electronic medical records (EMR) system was invaluable, offering a clear reference point for developing and refining our system.

Models

Utilizing MongoDB and Mongoose for our database presented a significant challenge in defining the appropriate schema and deciding the number of models necessary for efficient data management. After careful consideration, I concluded that creating three distinct models—notes, patients, and users—was the most effective approach to support our application's functionality and data integrity.
